ipad

The iPad is a brand of iOS and iPadOS-based tablet computers developed by Apple Inc. It is the successor of the Newton MessagePad and an unreleased prototype of the PowerBook Duo-based tablet computer. Apple concepted the iPad before the related iPhone, although the latter was developed and released first. Speculations of the original iPad began in 2002, and dealt with its development, operating system, and release, prior to its introduction on January 20, 2010. It consists of four lineups, the original iPad lineup and three flagships, iPad Mini, iPad Air, and iPad Pro. It initially used the iPhone's iOS operating system but was later replaced with an optimized derivation of iOS, iPadOS.Apple's first tablet computer and the iPad's predecessor was the Newton MessagePad, which was introduced in 1993. Apple also designed an alternative prototype of the PowerBook Duo-based tablet computer but decided not to sell it to avoid hurting MessagePad sales.[3][4] The Newton MessagePad was created under the leadership of John Sculley, who succeeded Apple co-founder Steve Jobs after he was forced out of Apple in 1985. The MessagePad was a technological failure, and its handwriting recognition often resulted in an indecipherable jumble of words. When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, he discontinued the MessagePad.[5] The development of an iPad tablet computer was first speculated in 2002 when Apple's associated manufacturing company Quanta leaked orders from Apple on wireless displays. Less than two weeks later after US Design Patent No. D504,889 leaked a concept design of the iPad in May 2005, the blogger Rob Bushway speculated that it would use a stripped-down version of OS X. Speculation then turned to Intel, who was visited by Apple to sought a chip to use on one of their potential products, speculatively the iPad. In September 2009, the Taiwan Economic News said that the iPad would be announced sometimes in February 2010 and cited "industry sources," but it was in fact, announced in January 2010.[6] The iPad's conception predated that of the iPhone, although the iPhone was chronologically developed and released before the iPad.[7][8] In 1991, Apple chief design officer Jonathan Ive devised an industrial design of a stylus-based tablet, the Macintosh Folio, which laid the foundation for a larger tablet prototype project codenamed K48 (the iPad) that Apple began in 2004. After seeking to develop the tablet first, Ive come to an agreement with Jobs that the iPhone was more important and reversed the order.[9][10][11] iPad The first generation of iPad was announced on January 20, 2010,[12] with pre-order began on March 12.[13] The Wi-Fi version was released in the United States on April 3, while the Wi-Fi- 3G version was released on April 30 [14] Apple released the first generation of iPad internationally on May 28, July 23, and September 17. Its multitouch-based display had a resolution of 1,024 by 768 pixels.[15] The second generation of iPad was announced on March 2, 2011 and released on March 11. Each generation of iPad requires a corresponding dock. A dock that included a physical keyboard was only supported for the original iPad,[56] but all generations are compatible with Bluetooth keyboards that also work with Macs and PCs. The iPad can be charged by a standalone power adapter ("wall charger") also used for iPods and iPhones, and a 10 W charger is included with the iPad.[citation needed] Apple sells a camera connection kit that consists of two separate adapters for the dock connector, one to USB Type A, the other an SD card reader. Adapter can be used to transfer photos and videos and to plug USB audio card or MIDI keyboard.[57] A third party sells an adapter that includes USB, SD, and MicroSD on a single unit.[58] An adapter to VGA connectors allows the iPad to work with external monitors and projectors. Another adapter mirrors the screen onto HDMI compatible devices in 1080p and works with all apps and rotations. Unlike other adapters, it allows the iPad to charge through another dock connector.[59] While the HDMI adapter was released with and advertised for the second generation iPad, it also works with the first-generation iPad, the iPhone 4, and the fourth generation iPod Touch.[60] Smart Covers are screen protectors that magnetically attach and align to the face of the iPad 2, 3, or 4. The cover has three folds which allow it to convert into a stand, which is also held together by magnets.[61] While original iPad owners could purchase a black case that included a similarly folding cover, the Smart Cover is meant to be more minimal, easily detachable, and protects only the screen. Smart Covers have a microfiber bottom that cleans the front of the iPad (which wakes up when the cover is removed). There are five different colors of both polyurethane and leather, with leather being more expensive. On September 1, it was announced the iPad would get iOS 4.2 by November 2010;[70] to fulfill this Apple released iOS 4.2.1 to the public on November 22.[71] Interface The interface is centred on the home screen, a graphical list of available applications. The home screen can be accessed at any time by a hardware button below the screen, closing an open application in the process.[72][failed verification] Users can also add and delete icons from the dock, which is the same on every home screen. Each home screen holds up to twenty icons, and the dock holds up to six icons. Users can delete Web Clips and third-party applications at any time, and may select only certain applications for transfer from iTunes. Apple's default programs, however, may not be removed.[citation needed] Almost all input is given through the touch screen, which understands complex gestures using multi-touch. The iPad's interaction techniques enable the user to move the content up or down by a touch-drag motion of the finger. For example, zooming in and out of web pages and photos is done by placing two fingers on the screen and spreading them farther apart or bringing them closer together, a gesture known as "pinching".[citation needed] Scrolling through a long list or menu is achieved by sliding a finger over the display from bottom to top, or vice versa to go back. In either case, the list moves as if it is pasted on the outer surface of a wheel, slowly decelerating as if affected by friction. In this way, the interface simulates the physics of a real object.[citation needed] Other user-centered interactive effects include horizontally sliding sub-selection, the vertically sliding keyboard and bookmarks menu, and widgets that turn around to allow settings to be configured on the other side. Menu bars are found at the top and bottom of the screen when necessary. Their options vary by program, but always follow a consistent style motif. In menu hierarchies, a "back" button in the top-left corner of the screen displays the name of the parent folder.[citation needed] Multimedia The layout of the music library is similar to an iPod or current[when?] Symbian S60 phones. The iPad can sort its media library by songs, artists, albums, videos, playlists, genres, composers, podcasts, audiobooks, and compilations. Options are always presented alphabetically, except in playlists, which retain their order from iTunes. The iPhone uses a large font that allows users plenty of room to touch their selection.[citation needed] Users can rotate their device horizontally to landscape mode to access Cover Flow, likewise this feature may be disabled if purposely wishing to view a screen from another angle. Like on iTunes, this feature shows the different album covers in a scroll-through photo library. Scrolling is achieved by swiping a finger across the screen. Alternatively, headset controls can be used to pause, play, skip, and repeat tracks.[citation needed] The iPad supports gapless playback.[73] Like the fifth-generation iPods introduced in 2005, the iPad can play digital video, allowing users to watch TV shows and movies in widescreen. Double-tapping switches between widescreen and fullscreen video playback.[citation needed] The iPad allows users to purchase and download songs from the iTunes Store directly to their iPad. It includes software that allows the user to upload, view, and email photos taken with the camera. The user zooms in and out of photos by sliding two fingers further apart or closer together, much like Safari. The Camera application also lets users view the camera roll, the pictures that have been taken with the iPad's camera. Those pictures are also available in the Photos application, along with any transferred from iPhoto or Aperture on a Mac, or Photoshop on a Windows PC.[citation needed] Internet connectivity The iPad can use Wi-Fi network trilateration from Skyhook Wireless to provide location information to applications such as Google Maps. The 3G model supports A-GPS to allow its position to be calculated with GPS or relative to nearby cellphone towers; it also has a black strip on the back to aid 3G reception.[74] The iPad has a headphone jack and a proprietary Apple dock connector, but no Ethernet or USB port.[55] However, the Apple Camera Connection Kit accessory provides two dock connector adapters for importing photos and videos via USB and SD memory cards.[citation needed] Applications The iPad comes with several applications, including Safari, Mail, Photos, Video, iPod, iTunes, App Store, Apple Books, Maps, Notes, Calendar, and Contacts.[75] Several are improved versions of applications developed for the iPhone or Mac. The apps currently missing are the weather, calculator, and health apps.[76] The iPad syncs with iTunes on a Mac or Windows PC.[77] Apple ported its iWork suite from the Mac to the iPad, and sells pared down versions of Pages, Numbers, and Keynote apps in the App Store.[78] Although the iPad is not designed to replace a mobile phone, a user can use a wired headset or the built-in speaker and microphone and place telephone calls over Wi-Fi or 3G using a VoIP application.[79] As of June 2012, there were about 225,000 iPad specific apps on the App Store.[80][needs update] In December 2010, Reuters reported that iPhone and iPad users have lodged a lawsuit against Apple alleging that some applications were passing their information to third party advertisers without consent.[81]]
